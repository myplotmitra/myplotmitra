How to Buy a Plot in Andhra Pradesh: A Step-by-Step Guide

Buying a plot is one of the biggest financial decisions most families make, and in Andhra Pradesh the process involves several distinct steps — checking approvals, verifying title, negotiating price, and completing registration. This guide walks through each step in order, with a focus on what buyers in Anantapur district specifically should check.

Step 1: Decide your budget and location

Start with a clear budget that includes not just the plot price but registration costs (roughly 7.5% of the property value in Andhra Pradesh — see our stamp duty guide), and if you plan to build, a rough construction budget too. Decide which town or area fits your needs — proximity to work, schools, and connectivity to National Highway 44 are common priorities for buyers around Anantapur.

Step 2: Shortlist verified listings

Browse listings from direct owners rather than relying only on word of mouth. On MyPlotMitra, look for the Verified Owner and Verified Property badges, which indicate our team has checked ownership and property documents. This doesn't replace your own due diligence, but it's a useful first filter.

Step 3: Check DTCP approval status

Before you get emotionally invested in a plot, confirm it is DTCP approved (or approved by the relevant local planning authority). Our companion guide, DTCP vs RERA Approval Explained, covers exactly what this means and how to verify it. An unapproved layout can cause serious problems later — from difficulty getting a home loan to trouble reselling.

Step 4: Verify title and documents

Ask the seller for: the original sale deed, encumbrance certificate (showing the property is free of loans or legal disputes), property tax receipts, and the approved layout plan. It's worth engaging a local property lawyer to review these documents — the cost is small compared to the risk of a title dispute after purchase.

Step 5: Negotiate and agree on price

Compare the seller's asking price against similar listings in the same area, and remember the government's official guideline value acts as a floor for stamp duty calculation regardless of what you actually pay — so extreme underquoting doesn't reduce your registration cost.

Step 6: Pay stamp duty and registration

Once you and the seller agree, the sale deed is prepared and stamp duty is paid — currently around 5% of the property's market or guideline value in Andhra Pradesh, plus 1% registration fee and 1.5% transfer duty (roughly 7.5% total for most sale deeds). See our full stamp duty and registration charges guide for exact current rates and a worked example.

Step 7: Register at the Sub-Registrar's Office

Both buyer and seller (or authorised representatives) need to be physically present at the Sub-Registrar's Office for biometric verification and execution of the sale deed. Bring Aadhaar, PAN, photographs, and all property documents. Once registered, the document becomes part of the permanent public record.

Step 8: Update records after registration

After registration, apply to transfer the property to your name in local municipal or panchayat records, and update property tax records so future tax bills come to you directly.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does the plot-buying process take in Andhra Pradesh?

It varies, but once you've finalised a plot with clear documents, registration itself can often be completed within a few days of scheduling an appointment at the Sub-Registrar's Office. Document verification and legal due diligence beforehand typically take longer.

Can I buy a plot in Anantapur without visiting in person?

We strongly recommend visiting the plot in person, or sending a trusted representative, before making any payment. Site verification badges on MyPlotMitra listings can help, but they don't replace seeing the property yourself.

This article is for general information only and reflects rules and rates believed accurate as of July 2026. Property laws, stamp duty, and approval rules can change — always verify current rates and requirements with the relevant government portal or a qualified professional before making a financial or legal decision.
